JLW Newsletter January 18, 2026
Energy Crisis

The Grid Cannot Hold

AI's insatiable hunger for electricity is breaking the power grid. This week, Washington intervened, grid operators drew lines, and Big Tech started building its own power plants.

01

The White House Just Told Big Tech to Pay for Its Own Power Grid

via AP News / The Spokesman-Review January 16, 2026

Here's a sentence you wouldn't have read five years ago: The White House is pressuring PJM Interconnection, the country's largest grid operator, to hold an emergency wholesale electricity auction. The goal? Force tech companies to subsidize the construction of new power plants before AI training runs start browning out the Eastern Seaboard.

The administration's position is blunt: "We need to make sure that the American family doesn't pay for the energy needs of Big Tech." This isn't just rhetoric. Northeastern governors are backing the proposal, watching nervously as Google, Microsoft, and Meta gobble up every available megawatt while residential bills climb.

The political calculus is straightforward: if OpenAI wants to train GPT-6, it can fund the gas plants or nuclear reactors needed to power it. What's fascinating is that this represents the first time the federal government has explicitly framed AI infrastructure as a private cost that shouldn't be socialized. The era of unlimited grid access for the asking is over.

02

Bring Your Own Gigawatts: PJM's Ultimatum to Data Centers

via Virginia Business January 16, 2026

PJM Interconnection isn't waiting for Washington to act. The grid operator that serves 65 million people across 13 states dropped a bombshell proposal this week: large data centers may need to provide their own power generation or face mandatory curtailment during peak stress events.

Translation: if you want to run a 500 MW AI training cluster in Virginia, you'd better arrive with your own gas turbines or battery arrays. Otherwise, when temperatures spike and the grid groans, your servers get switched off first.

The new rule: "Data centers may need to bring their own generation or be prepared to be cut off during emergencies." — PJM Interconnection proposal

This is a seismic shift in how we think about cloud infrastructure. For two decades, hyperscalers like AWS and Azure have treated grid power as an infinite resource—pay the utility bill, get the electrons. PJM's proposal redefines data centers from customers to quasi-utilities, responsible for their own reliability. The implications ripple through every AI roadmap on the planet.

03

Air Cooling Is Dead. Long Live the Liquid.

via Lombard Odier / Data Center Digest January 15, 2026

Here's a technical milestone that doesn't get the attention it deserves: liquid cooling has officially replaced air cooling as the standard for new AI data centers. Industry analysis released this week confirms what anyone watching NVIDIA's thermal specs already knew—rack densities approaching 100kW make fans and HVAC systems physically inadequate.

The numbers are stark. Direct-to-chip liquid cooling cuts cooling energy costs by up to 15%. At the scale of a hyperscale AI facility, that's tens of millions of dollars annually. But the transition isn't free: existing facilities need complete retrofits, and entirely new supply chains for coolants, pumps, and precision plumbing must materialize.

The quote that captures the moment: "Liquid cooling is no longer a niche for supercomputers; it is the baseline for the AI era." Every data center built before 2024 is now technically obsolete. The hardware architecture of the entire industry is being rewritten in real-time.

04

Using AI to Cool the Machines That Run AI

via GlobeNewswire January 14, 2026

There's a recursive irony in using artificial intelligence to manage the cooling systems of artificial intelligence data centers. 3 E Network Technology unveiled exactly that this week: an AI-native facility management system that claims to reduce annual Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) by 8% to 15%.

The system uses real-time analysis of compute loads, weather data, and thermal sensors to dynamically adjust cooling before hotspots develop. Traditional cooling systems operate reactively—temperature rises, chillers spin up. This approach predicts and pre-positions, shaving critical percentage points off energy consumption.

When raw power becomes the binding constraint on AI progress, efficiency gains become the fastest path forward. You can't conjure new transmission lines overnight, but you can squeeze more FLOPS out of every megawatt. Expect every major operator to deploy similar systems within 18 months.

05

The Real Bottleneck Isn't Chips—It's Permission to Plug In

via Network World January 13, 2026

Google's global head of sustainability said something this week that should terrify anyone betting on near-term AI scaling: utility connection wait times have ballooned to 4-10 years. In one particularly absurd case, an interconnection request received a 12-year timeline—just for the study phase.

"The U.S. transmission grid was not designed for the digital economy we are building today." That's not advocacy group rhetoric—that's Google, explaining why it's exploring "co-location" strategies to bypass the queue by building directly next to power plants.

This reframes the entire AI hardware conversation. We've spent three years obsessing over NVIDIA allocations, TSMC capacity, and HBM supply chains. Turns out the real constraint isn't semiconductors—it's permission to use electricity. Physical infrastructure trumps digital dreams.

06

Meta Just Became America's Biggest Corporate Nuclear Customer

via World Nuclear News / ESG Today January 12, 2026

Meta announced agreements with TerraPower, Oklo, and Vistra to secure up to 6.6 gigawatts of nuclear energy capacity by 2035. That's not a typo. 6.6 GW—enough to power roughly 5 million homes, or train a lot of LLaMA successors.

The deal includes funding for up to eight small modular reactors (SMRs) using TerraPower's Natrium technology. First reactors target operation in the early 2030s. As Joel Kaplan put it: "These agreements make Meta one of the most significant corporate purchasers of nuclear energy in American history."

The nuclear renaissance everyone predicted a decade ago is finally arriving—not because of climate policy, but because AI companies need baseload power that solar and wind can't reliably provide at scale. Sometimes the future arrives for unexpected reasons.

The Watt Is Mightier Than the Chip

We've spent years asking "when will AI reach human-level intelligence?" The more urgent question: "where will the electricity come from?" This week made clear that the AI boom is now an energy story. Every model architecture breakthrough, every new training paradigm, every efficiency gain—all of it only matters if you can plug it in. The companies that secure power win. The ones that don't will watch from the brownout.
